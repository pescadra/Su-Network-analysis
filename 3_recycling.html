Entrepreneurial Recycling Analysis¶

Beyond the Exit Myth: Optimizing Ecosystem Value¶

Key Question¶

Are exits always good for the Flanders ecosystem?

No - the literature shows that not all exits are equal, and an optimal ecosystem requires a balanced portfolio:

  • Some exits (for liquidity and legitimacy)
  • Some staying unicorns/scale-ups (for anchoring and depth)
  • Strong recycling mechanisms (to close the loop)

What We Analyze¶

  1. Exit Type Classification: Local vs European vs Foreign multinational M&A
  2. Founder Recycling: Do exited founders re-enter as angels or new founders?
  3. Scale-Up Anchors: Which startups stay and build ecosystem capacity?
  4. Optimal Portfolio: What mix maximizes long-term ecosystem value?

Policy Implication¶

Goal: Maximize recycled value, not exit value

Framework: Based on Spigel & Vinodrai (2020), Mason & Harrison (2006), Startup Genome (2023)

Literature Review: Entrepreneurial Recycling¶

Core Concept: Recycling > Exits¶

Spigel & Vinodrai (2020) - "Entrepreneurial Recycling and Ecosystem Resilience":

"Even when anchor firms collapse or exit, ecosystems remain resilient if recycling channels exist. Institutional capacity (networks, matching, finance) determines outcomes."

Mason & Harrison (2006) - "Business Angel Recycling":

"Successful exits are the largest source of angel capital. 60-70% of angels are exited entrepreneurs."

Exit Types Matter¶

European Longitudinal Study (45 cities, 20 years):

  • Acquisitions significantly increase:
    • Follow-on investment activity
    • New venture creation
  • IPOs have smaller, marginal effects
  • ➜ Local acquisitions > foreign acquisitions for ecosystem impact

Small Economy Evidence (Estonia, Finland):

  • Over-optimization for exits → talent drain
  • Balance with anchors → sustained ecosystem

Optimal Portfolio Theory¶

Startup Genome (2023) - Ecosystem Value Framework:

Ecosystem Value = Unicorn Value + Exit Value + Recycling Multiplier

Avnimelech & Teubal (2006) - Israeli Ecosystem:

  • Early exits to US multinationals weakened local ecosystem
  • Later policy: encourage staying or local M&A
  • Result: Stronger second-generation firms

Recycling Mechanisms¶

What Gets Recycled:

  1. Capital: Exited founders become angel investors
  2. Experience: Serial entrepreneurs start new ventures
  3. Knowledge: Ex-employees join or found startups
  4. Networks: Ecosystem connectors and mentors

Recycling Intensity varies by:

  • Acquirer geography (local > foreign)
  • Exit timing (mature > premature)
  • Founder embedment in local ecosystem

Policy Relevance for Flanders¶

As a small, open economy, Flanders needs:

  • ✅ Portfolio approach (not exit maximization)
  • ✅ Local/European M&A incentives
  • ✅ Anchor firm support (unicorns staying)
  • ✅ Recycling infrastructure (angel networks, founder programs)
  • ❌ Over-dependence on foreign multinational acquisitions

Framework 1: Spigel & Vinodrai (2020) - "Entrepreneurial Recycling"¶

Their Framework:

"Ecosystems remain resilient if recycling channels exist. Institutional capacity (networks, matching, finance) determines outcomes."

What They Measured:

  • Actual founder re-entry rates
  • Alumni networks from exited/failed firms
  • Institutional infrastructure (angel networks, incubators)
  • Knowledge/capital flow from exits back to ecosystem

What We Measured:

  • ✅ Exit types (proxy for recycling potential) — 153 exits classified by geography
  • ✅ Portfolio balance (exits vs anchors) — 712 TRUE startups
  • ❌ MISSING: Actual founder re-entry behavior
  • ❌ MISSING: Do exited founders become angels?
  • ❌ MISSING: Time to re-entry
  • ❌ MISSING: Institutional capacity assessment

Our Interpretation:

  • 62% Belgisch acquisitions (94 of 151 classified) → moderate potential for capital to stay local
  • But we don't know if this actually translates to recycling
  • Need follow-up: Track exited founders in Dealroom investor lists

Framework 2: Mason & Harrison (2006) - "Business Angel Recycling"¶

Their Benchmark (UK context):

"60-70% of angels are exited entrepreneurs."

What They Measured:

  • Angel investor backgrounds
  • % with prior exit experience
  • Source of angel capital (exit proceeds)
  • Time from exit to angel investing

What We Measured:

  • ✅ Number of exits: 153 from 5 sources (2014–2026)
  • ✅ Exit type (acquirer geography): 61% Belgisch, 37% Buitenlands (of 151 classified)
  • ❌ MISSING: Which investors in our network are exited founders?
  • ❌ MISSING: % of our 722 investors who had prior exits
  • ❌ MISSING: Whether exited founders re-entered as angels

What We CAN Infer:

  • We have 722 investors in the Dealroom data (2011–2026)
  • Of these, ~312 are likely individuals/angels (cell 16 name-pattern analysis: 42% individuals + <1% angel networks)
  • We identified 153 exits across 6 types
  • If Mason & Harrison's 60-70% holds for the angel pool:
    • Expected: ~187–218 of our ~312 angel-like investors should be exited entrepreneurs
    • Our 153 exits provide a plausible supply pool, but would need to include older/pre-2014 exits too
  • Note: The 60-70% benchmark applies to angel investors specifically, not all investors (which include VCs, corporates, government funds)

Data Limitation:

  • Mason & Harrison's 60-70% benchmark is UK-specific — Flanders may differ
  • Need founder-level data (not available in current dataset)
  • Investor backgrounds not included in Dealroom export

FOUNDER RECYCLING ANALYSIS¶

Testing Mason & Harrison (2006) Benchmark: 60-70% of Angels Are Exited Entrepreneurs¶

Critical Question: Do our exited founders actually recycle back into the ecosystem as investors?

Method:

  1. Extract all unique investor names from Dealroom
  2. Cross-reference with exited startup names (from acquisitions_enriched)

🔄 CRITICAL CORRECTION: Dealroom Exits Analysis¶

NBB Data Reveals Only Belgian Shareholders — Not Foreign Acquirers¶

Problem Identified: The NBB (Nationale Bank België) shareholder data only captures Belgian legal entities as shareholders. When a foreign company (e.g., ServiceNow, Uber, GE Healthcare) acquires a Flemish startup, this is invisible in NBB data unless the acquirer creates a Belgian subsidiary.

Impact: The earlier finding of "91% local exits" was an artefact of data blindness, not a reflection of reality.

Solution: Dealroom maintains a dedicated exits export with actual acquirer names, types, and HQ locations.
